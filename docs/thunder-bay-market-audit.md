## Step 1 — Market Research

### Population & Demographics

- **City population:** ~110,000 (city proper); ~121,000 (census metropolitan area)
- **Trend:** Flat to slightly declining over the past two decades, now stabilized
- **Age skew:** Older than Ontario average — high median age, significant retiree population
- **Heritage communities:** Large Finnish and Italian populations, significant Indigenous population (~12-13%)
- **Households:** ~50,000-52,000 in the metro area
- **Housing stock:** Predominantly older homes (1950s-1980s build) — aging furnaces and HVAC systems that need replacement. This is a strong demand driver

### Average Revenue — Small HVAC Contractors

| Size | Employees | Revenue Range |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Micro | 1-4 | $150K - $500K/yr |
| Small | 5-15 | $500K - $2M/yr |
| Mid-size | 15-30 | $2M - $5M/yr |

- Northern Ontario revenues run 10-20% lower than GTA equivalents
- Revenue is heavily winter-weighted: December through February = 40-50% of annual revenue
- Median for a typical 3-8 person shop: $500K-$800K

### Seasonal Patterns

**Heating season:** October through April (7 months — one of the longest in Ontario)

| Period | Call Pattern |
|--------|------------|
| September-October | Pre-season maintenance surge, system startups |
| November | After-hours calls start climbing |
| December-February | **Peak period** — 2-4x normal call volume |
| Christmas/New Year | **Highest spike** — contractors take time off, furnaces don't |
| March-April | Tapering but still significant |
| May-August | Lower volume, growing A/C demand |

**Temperature context:**
- Average January temp: -14.8°C (lows to -21°C average, extremes to -40°C)
- Heating Degree Days: ~5,400-5,700 annually (Toronto = ~3,500 for comparison)
- Average snowfall: 175-190 cm/year
- Regular wind chills of -30°C to -45°C in January/February
- Multiple cold snaps per winter with temps below -25°C for 3+ consecutive days

**Emergency call implications:**
- Furnace failures at -35°C are life-threatening — not a "wait until Monday" situation
- First major cold snap of each season generates a surge (systems fail on startup after sitting dormant)
- A single cold snap night can generate 3-5x normal call volume
- After-hours calls = 20-35% of all HVAC calls normally, 40-50% during cold snaps

### Labour Shortages

This is the strongest market tailwind for Northern Automation:

- Ontario projected 100,000+ skilled trades shortage by 2025 — Northern Ontario hit hardest
- Average age of skilled tradespeople in Northern Ontario: mid-to-late 40s, approaching retirement
- Young apprentices train in GTA/Ottawa and stay there
- Companies run too lean to staff a receptionist or after-hours dispatcher
- **Owner-operators are answering phones while on job sites** — this is who we're selling to
- AI answering is not replacing jobs — it's filling a gap that literally cannot be filled with people

### Local Culture & Communication Style

- **Relationship-driven:** People do business with people they know and trust
- **Word-of-mouth is king:** A city of 110K functions like a small town for reputation
- **Buy local:** Strong loyalty to local businesses
- **Straight talk:** Northern Ontario business owners value directness and reliability over flashy marketing
- **Cost-conscious but quality-aware:** They'll pay for reliability, but they don't want to feel sold to
- **Community-minded:** Businesses expected to be involved locally
- **Seasonal rhythm:** Entire economy pulses with seasons — they understand urgency cycles

### What Builds Trust / What They Distrust

**Trust builders:**
- Local company (Thunder Bay-based)
- Plain language, no corporate speak
- Practical ROI — "this pays for itself with one saved call"
- Free trial with no credit card trap
- Quick setup — they don't have time for complicated onboarding
- Understanding of their actual work (winter emergencies, being on the roof when the phone rings)

**Trust destroyers:**
- Slick marketing language / corporate buzzwords
- Pushy sales tactics
- Long contracts or hidden fees
- Anything that feels like a Toronto company selling to "the regions"
- Fake testimonials or exaggerated claims
- Complexity — if it's not simple, they won't use it

### Business Associations & Networks

- **Thunder Bay Chamber of Commerce** — primary local business association, Red River Road
- **Northwestern Ontario Associated Chambers of Commerce (NOACC)** — regional umbrella
- **Thunder Bay Construction Association** — directly relevant trades group
- **Northwestern Ontario Mechanical Contractors Association** — HVAC-specific
- **Ontario HVAC/R Association** — provincial trade body
- **HRAI (Heating, Refrigeration and Air Conditioning Institute of Canada)** — national
- **Northern Ontario Heritage Fund Corporation (NOHFC)** — business grants/incentives (potential marketing angle)
- **FedNor** — federal Northern Ontario business support

## Step 2 — Buyer Persona

### "Northern Mike" — Target Buyer Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Age** | 38-58 |
| **Gender** | ~90% male |
| **Business size** | 1-8 employees |
| **Revenue** | $200K-$1M/year |
| **Role** | Owner-operator or owner with a small crew |
| **Education** | Trade school / apprenticeship, possibly college |
| **Tech comfort** | Uses a smartphone daily, has a basic website or Google listing, not tech-savvy beyond that |

**How they make purchasing decisions:**
- Word of mouth from other contractors they respect
- Tries it themselves before committing (free trial is critical)
- Needs to see it work, not read about it working
- Decides in 1-2 conversations — not a long sales cycle
- Price-sensitive but understands value if framed as ROI
- "What's it gonna cost me and what do I get" — that's the whole conversation

**Daily reality:**
- Up at 6 AM, on a job site by 7:30
- Phone rings while they're elbow-deep in a furnace
- Can't answer, doesn't see the voicemail until 4 PM, customer already called someone else
- Evenings spent doing quotes, invoicing, ordering parts
- During cold snaps: working 14-16 hour days, phone ringing constantly
- No receptionist, no office staff — maybe a spouse helps with bookkeeping
- They know they're losing calls. They just don't have a solution that isn't "hire someone I can't find"

**What they care about most:**
1. Not losing jobs to competitors
2. Not missing emergency calls (liability + reputation)
3. Having some semblance of work-life balance
4. Keeping their reputation — they built it over years
5. Keeping things simple — zero tolerance for complicated tech

**Language and tone that resonates:**
- Direct, no-filler: "You miss calls. We answer them. You get more jobs."
- Problem-solution: "Your phone rings at 2 AM when it's -35. Do you answer it?"
- Local framing: "Built in Thunder Bay" carries real weight
- Trades vernacular: "on the job," "booked up," "no-heat call," "shoulder season"
- Avoid: "leverage," "optimize," "solution," "innovative," "cutting-edge," "AI-powered" (say what it does, not what it is)

## Step 3 — Landing Page Optimization (thunder-bay.html)

### Recommended Copy Direction

**Hero headline:** "Your Phone Rings at 2 AM. It's -35. Who's Answering?"
**Subheadline:** "Northern Automation answers every call to your business — nights, weekends, blizzards — so you never lose a job to a missed call again."

**Key copy principles:**
- Lead with the weather. Every Thunder Bay contractor has a visceral reaction to "-35°C"
- Frame around loss, not gain: "You're losing $50K+ in missed calls every year" hits harder than "make more money"
- Position the product as "your after-hours dispatcher" not "AI technology"
- Every section should answer: "What does this actually do for me?"

### Imagery Direction

**What would work:**
- A snow-covered Thunder Bay residential street at dusk — porch lights on, snow falling, the feeling of "someone's furnace just died"
- A contractor's phone screen showing missed calls (relatable, not staged)
- The Sleeping Giant or Thunder Bay skyline in winter — instant local recognition
- A work van in a snowy driveway — the reality of the job

**What to avoid:**
- Stock photos of people in headsets (screams "call centre")
- Generic "happy business owner" corporate stock
- Anything that looks like it came from a marketing agency in Toronto
- Overly polished or unrealistic imagery

### FAQ Tone

Rewrite all FAQ answers as if a contractor is asking another contractor. No corporate hedging.

### CTA Copy

- Primary: "Try It Free for 14 Days" (not "Start Your Free Trial" — too corporate)
- Secondary: "Hear It Handle a Call" (link to demo)
- Avoid: "Get Started," "Learn More," "Schedule a Demo"

## Step 4 — Outbound Call Script Optimization

### Current Script Adjustments for Thunder Bay HVAC Contractors

**Opening line (recommended):**
> "Hi [name], this is [agent] from Northern Automation — we're a Thunder Bay company. I'll be quick because I know you're probably on a job. We built an AI that answers your business phone 24/7 so you stop losing calls. Got 30 seconds?"

**Why this works:**
- "Thunder Bay company" — instant local credibility
- "I'll be quick because I know you're probably on a job" — shows understanding of their reality
- "Stop losing calls" — pain point, not feature
- "Got 30 seconds?" — respects their time, low commitment ask

**Pain point framing:**
- "How many calls do you think you missed last heating season?"
- "When it hits -35 and your phone's going off at 2 AM, are you picking up every one?"
- "What happens when you're on a job and the phone rings?"

**Objection handling for this audience:**

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "I answer my own phone" | "I hear you. What about when you're on the roof or under a unit? What about 2 AM? We just catch the ones you can't." |
| "My customers want to talk to a real person" | "They think they are. It sounds like a receptionist, uses your business name, asks the right questions. Most callers don't know the difference." |
| "I don't need it in the summer" | "Fair enough. Most of our guys use it hardest October through March. You can try it free for 14 days — test it before heating season." |
| "How much?" | "$197 a month — less than what one missed emergency call would've made you. And the first 14 days are free." |
| "I'm not a tech guy" | "Neither are most of our customers. You forward your number, we do the rest. Takes about a minute." |
| "I'll think about it" | "No problem. Tell you what — want me to just set up the trial? It's free, no credit card, and you can hear it handle a call before you decide anything." |

**Tone and pacing:**
- Slightly slower pace than a Toronto sales call — Northern Ontario conversations breathe more
- Casual but respectful — like talking to another tradesperson, not a customer
- Don't oversell — state the facts, let them decide
- Use "we" and "our guys" — community language
- If they want to chat about the weather or the Thunderwolves, let them — relationship matters

## Summary

Thunder Bay is an ideal beachhead market for Northern Automation:

- **Acute pain point:** 7-month heating season with life-threatening cold creates urgent need for 24/7 phone coverage
- **Labour shortage:** Contractors can't hire — AI answering fills an unfillable gap
- **Small market advantage:** 25-35 HVAC targets means complete market coverage is achievable
- **Local trust:** "Built in Thunder Bay" is a genuine competitive advantage that no outside competitor can match
- **Price point works:** $197/month < the value of one missed emergency call
- **Word of mouth:** Land 3-5 happy customers and the market will start selling itself

The landing page, call scripts, and outreach should all emphasize: local, practical, no-nonsense, winter-tough, and genuinely useful.
